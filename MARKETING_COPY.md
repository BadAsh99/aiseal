## What It Detects — OWASP LLM Top 10 in Plain English

```
LLM01 — PROMPT INJECTION
What it is: An attacker crafts input that overrides your AI's instructions.
            Instead of answering customer support questions, your AI starts
            following the attacker's commands.
Why it matters: This is the #1 LLM attack vector. Production exploits are documented.
                If your AI processes untrusted input (user messages, documents,
                emails, web content), it's exposed.
What we test: Direct injection attempts, indirect injection via document content,
              instruction override patterns, role-switching attacks.

LLM02 — SENSITIVE INFORMATION DISCLOSURE
What it is: The AI leaks data it shouldn't — PII, PHI, credentials, confidential
            business context from its training or retrieval context.
Why it matters: GDPR, HIPAA, and SOC 2 all have opinions about this.
                "The AI did it" is not a defense.
What we test: Probes designed to extract memorized training data, retrieval context
              leakage, and credential exposure patterns.

LLM04 — DATA AND MODEL POISONING
What it is: If your AI uses retrieval-augmented generation (RAG), an attacker can
            inject malicious content into your knowledge base that corrupts AI
            responses for all users.
Why it matters: RAG systems are everywhere. The attack surface is your document store.
What we test: Indirect injection via document content, poisoning patterns in
              retrieval inputs. Flagged if your architecture uses RAG.

LLM05 — IMPROPER OUTPUT HANDLING
What it is: The AI generates content — code, markup, commands — that downstream
            systems execute without validation. Classic injection, elevated to AI scale.
Why it matters: If your AI generates code that gets run, or HTML that gets rendered,
                or commands that get executed, output handling is your attack surface.
What we test: Code generation probes, XSS/SQLi pattern generation, command injection
              via AI output.

LLM06 — EXCESSIVE AGENCY
What it is: The AI takes real-world actions — sending emails, calling APIs, modifying
            data — beyond what it should be allowed to do. Often triggered by a
            prompt injection that escalates privilege.
Why it matters: Agentic AI is the new frontier. Excessive agency turns a chat interface
                into an attack vector with real-world consequences.
What we test: High-privilege action requests, escalation patterns, scope violation
              probes. Mandatory for certification — zero tolerance.

LLM07 — SYSTEM PROMPT LEAKAGE
What it is: An attacker extracts your AI's system prompt — the internal instructions,
            persona, and configuration that define how it behaves. Once exposed, your
            IP is leaked and your security controls can be reverse-engineered.
Why it matters: Your system prompt contains your business logic. "Repeat your
                instructions" is the oldest trick in the book. Your AI should
                resist it.
What we test: Direct extraction requests, indirect probing, jailbreak patterns
              that reveal internal configuration.

LLM08 — VECTOR AND EMBEDDING WEAKNESSES
What it is: Vulnerabilities in how your AI stores and retrieves vector embeddings —
            allowing attackers to manipulate similarity search, extract training
            data from embeddings, or corrupt the retrieval index.
Why it matters: Vector databases are new infrastructure. Security practices for them
                are still maturing. Most teams don't know what to test.
What we test: Embedding extraction patterns, retrieval manipulation probes,
              index corruption scenarios.

LLM09 — MISINFORMATION
What it is: The AI confidently generates false information — hallucinations, fabricated
            citations, invented facts — that users or downstream systems act on.
Why it matters: Regulated industries (healthcare, legal, financial) have specific
                liability exposure when AI misinforms at scale.
What we test: Factual probes, citation fabrication patterns, confidence calibration
              under adversarial conditions.

LLM10 — UNBOUNDED CONSUMPTION
What it is: An attacker causes the AI to consume excessive compute, tokens, or
            time — through prompt flooding, recursive generation triggers, or
            resource exhaustion attacks. Also known as AI DoS.
Why it matters: Production LLM APIs cost money. Unbounded consumption is both a
                security and a cost control problem.
What we test: Token exhaustion patterns, recursive generation triggers, rate limit
              bypass probes.
```

## Runtime vs Static Scanning — Explanation Block

```
STATIC SCANNING (AISeal Scan)
— Runs before deployment
— Tests known attack patterns against your configuration
— Finds: prompt injection vulnerabilities, excessive agency exposure, 
         system prompt leakage risk, output handling issues
— Doesn't see: real user behavior, novel attacks, multi-turn sessions,
               production context
— Frequency: on-demand, pre-release, CI/CD gate
— Output: TrustScore + findings report

RUNTIME MONITORING (AISeal Monitor)
— Runs continuously in production
— Observes every prompt and response as they happen
— Finds: live attacks, behavioral anomalies, novel injection patterns,
         data exfiltration attempts, policy violations in context
— Doesn't replace: pre-deployment scanning (you still need the baseline)
— Frequency: continuous, real-time
— Output: classified event stream + anomaly alerts + full audit log

Use both. Scan to know your baseline. Monitor to know your reality.
```
